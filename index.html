Wallet Platform · Strategic Initiative

From instrument storage
to a true digital wallet.

Wallet 1.0 stores cards, bank accounts, and PayPal. That's it — no access control, no permissions, no extensibility. Wallet 2.0 reimagines the wallet as an identity container: holding instruments and permissions together so that who can use what, for what purpose, and for how long is governed by design — not assumed.

Why — The Problems

Four shortcomings
driving Wallet 2.0

Click each to expand.

🪪
Trust & Identity Fragmentation
61% of wallets are unlinked — we can't consistently recognize or protect the same customer

Only 39% of wallets (93M) are linked to a verified Intuit profile. The remaining 61% (141M) are unlinked identities created by businesses on behalf of their customers. This fragmentation limits our ability to consistently personalize experiences, apply protections, and resolve support issues — driving higher fraud exposure and "ownership" ambiguity (e.g., who actually controls this wallet or instrument?).

For SMBs, the result is an inconsistent ability to recognize and serve the same customer across touchpoints, which creates operational friction and reduces conversion to saved-payment experiences. For end consumers, it shows up as confusing and repetitive payment journeys — saved methods don't reliably carry across invoices, checkout links, and pay-now flows, forcing re-entry and creating uncertainty about which identity they're transacting under.

🔒
Limited Management & Visibility
No RBAC, no spending limits, no ability for users to claim or control their own instruments

Customers lack the ability to view and manage the specific permissions and usages of their stored instruments. Users are currently unable to claim instruments previously added by SMBs on their behalf. Business owners have no robust way to grant role-based access to payment instruments or set specific spending limits for employees. There is no concept of: "Use my Chase Bank ending in XXX124 for Payroll only."

🏦
Vault-Centric Design
Cards, bank accounts, PayPal only — adding new instrument types requires re-architecture

The current Wallet Service functions primarily as a static vault rather than an extensible platform. It was built for a fixed set of instrument types — cards, bank accounts, and PayPal — and the data model doesn't accommodate new types like balance accounts or cryptocurrency without significant re-architecture. Every new instrument type becomes a custom project rather than a configuration.

🔀
Inconsistent Experiences
Redundant development across teams produces divergent UX — even for basics like nickname handling

Without a true platform layer, teams build their own payment management logic independently. The result: redundant development efforts across multiple product teams that produce inconsistent customer experiences. Something as basic as how instrument nicknames are handled differs between surfaces. Wallet 2.0 consolidates these into platform-level capabilities so teams get consistency for free.

What — Wallet 2.0

Wallets as identity containers

Not static card or bank vaults. Wallets in 2.0 hold instruments and permissions together — so that who can use what, for what purpose, and for how long is governed by design rather than assumed. Three capabilities make this real:

🔧
Payment-Agnostic Instruments
Support for any instrument type — not just cards, bank accounts, and PayPal. Balance accounts, crypto, and future types are first-class citizens. New instruments are configuration, not custom builds.
🛡️
Granular Access Control
Generate credentials scoped to a specific merchant, amount, or payment type. Business owners can grant role-based access and set spending limits. Users can claim instruments added on their behalf.
🪪
Linked Identity Layer
Instruments and verified identity travel together. Closes the 61% unlinked gap — so Intuit can consistently recognize, protect, and serve the same customer across all touchpoints and products.
Where — Launch Partners

Starting with three partners

Wallet 2.0 will roll out incrementally. These three use cases are our first proving grounds — chosen because they surface the platform's capabilities most directly.

Launch Partner 1
IES Shared Vendor
The unlock: Sharing a single vendor payment method across multiple entities within an IES account — without creating duplicate instruments or requiring per-entity re-entry. Directly enabled by Wallet 2.0's cross-entity instrument model.
Launch Partner 2
Bill Pay Credit Card
The unlock: Enabling credit card as a payment instrument within Bill Pay — a new instrument type that the current vault-centric Wallet 1.0 cannot cleanly accommodate. Validates the payment-agnostic instrument model.
Launch Partner 3
WFS Wallet
The unlock: Migrating the WFS (Workforce Solutions) wallet onto the Wallet 2.0 platform — bringing identity linking, access control, and extensibility to the WFS money movement surface.

FAQ

Common questions

Answers to what PMs and engineering leads typically ask.

What does this mean for my team's roadmap?

If your team is building payment flows today, Wallet 2.0 will give you a permission model, identity linking, and instrument extensibility out of the box — without having to build your own access logic. Migration will be phased; IES Shared Vendor, Bill Pay CC, and WFS Wallet are first. Your team will get migration guidance and support before any action is required.

Do my current integrations break?

No breaking changes without prior coordination and migration support. Wallet 2.0 is being built to be adopted incrementally. The launch phase focuses on net-new capabilities (shared vendor instruments, new instrument types, RBAC) rather than ripping out existing integrations. Existing Wallet 1.0 integrations will continue to function while migration paths are established.

When does my team need to act?

Right now: stay informed and flag use cases where Wallet 2.0 capabilities (access control, new instrument types, cross-entity sharing) would unblock your team. The Wallet Platform team wants to hear about your needs now so they can prioritize migration sequencing. Reach out to the contacts below to get on the radar.

Who owns Wallet 2.0 and who do I contact?

The Wallet Platform team owns the initiative. See the Contacts section below for the right people by area. For general questions, start with the PM or Engineering Manager listed there.

Why these three launch partners first?

IES Shared Vendor, Bill Pay CC, and WFS Wallet were selected because they each exercise a distinct capability pillar of Wallet 2.0 — cross-entity instrument sharing, new instrument type support, and identity + access control respectively. Proving all three in parallel de-risks the platform before broader adoption.
